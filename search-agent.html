SearchAgent: Optimizing Commercial Due Diligence through Brief-Aware AI

I led the evolution of SearchAgent from a functional MVP into a brief-aware discovery engine — shifting expert search from manual database filtering to AI-driven semantic ranking.

Context

The Race to the "First List"

In Commercial Due Diligence (CDD), speed is the competitive advantage. Private Equity clients measure Dialectica by Time-to-First-List — how fast the team identifies and presents relevant experts after a project launches. Winning PE accounts depends on delivering the fastest first list. SearchAgent is the internal tool that powers expert discovery for ~700+ Client Service associates worldwide.

Strategic Challenge

From Data Filterer to Project Strategist

SearchAgent V2 was a planned evolution, not a failure-driven redesign. V1 centralised fragmented discovery into a single database — but it remained a high-effort search tool requiring associates to manually scan hundreds of raw results. V2 introduced a semantic layer that interprets the project brief, moving users from manual retrieval to predictive discovery.

V1 — Validating the MVP
  • Moved expert discovery from LinkedIn into an internal ecosystem
  • Established a functional database of competitor companies and former employees
  • Key learning: not ranking by project relevance created cognitive load — associates spent hours manually vetting for relevance. Incentive was very helpful.
V2 — Introducing Brief-Awareness
  • Added a Brief-Aware Ranking algorithm on top of the existing expert pool
  • Keep "Formers" ranked by seniority, tenure, and recency of departure, as well as incentive; "Currents" ranked by seniority and tenure; and add a new "Suggested" list ranking experts by project relevance — initially including all experts from both "Currents" and "Formers", with the plan of evolving this into a curated shortlist
  • Value shift: instead of scrolling 300+ profiles, users start from the "Suggested" list with the most valuable experts in the first 1–2 pages, with the plan of replacing this with a curated shortlist in V3
The Process

Phase 1 — Stabilise & Explore

Months 1–6: Stabilise V1 while defining SearchAgent's future

When I became UX lead for the Automation stream, V1 was the operational baseline. I shipped quick-win improvements — a heuristic audit, new filters for list customisation, and an exploration to bring Customer Lists into V1 — while building the evidence base for V2. In parallel, I partnered with Product, Data Science, and Engineering to define SearchAgent's strategic direction. We prototyped multi-perspective ideas and bulk-action workflows, converging on the two highest-impact perspective types: Competitors and Customers.

Insight

Early exploration revealed that ranking — not scope — was the core friction. Associates didn't need more data; they needed better signal.

Phase 2 — Evidence-Led Convergence

Research study with 15 global users — MBB account holders and project managers

To validate V2 concepts, I led a global research study. Through technical negotiation with Engineering, we identified that Confirmed Customer Lists would delay launch by months — and research confirmed it wasn't a top user need. I steered the design toward a consolidated Suggested list for fast cross-project vetting, while keeping confirmed customers within competitors as approached in V1.

70% of MBB account holders said a "ready-to-use shortlist" would be a game-changer for speed.

Finding

Users define quality by expert recency (last 12 months) and previous screening by colleagues.

"I would expect to see experts screened in previous projects... recently screened, e.g., the last 6 months."

Design Decision

Ranking logic surfaces highest-incentive and most-consulted experts first. For customer expert profiles specifically, ranking prioritises product/service familiarity — current users ranked first — and quote recency.

Finding

80% of users are company-agnostic — they need any customer of a competitor. Only 20% require deep dives into specific entities.

"In 80% of cases, the client doesn't care for the specific customer companies, they just need customer profiles of X competitors."

Design Decision

Maintained a consolidated Customer View under each competitor while enabling drill-downs for targeted recruitment.

Finding

PMs prioritise a consolidated ready-to-send list for speed. Associates require company-specific lists for team delegation.

"This is a game changer... Having for example a 15-people ready-to-send FL would be great."

Design Decision

The Suggested tab re-ranks the expert pool by brief match, providing a curated starting point. The full ready-to-send shortlist is planned for V3.

Finding

Associates leave the platform to use Gemini or Google for company discovery, fragmenting the workflow and losing context.

"I use Gemini to ask for a common ground... and then ask for an expanded list..."

Design Decision

Integrated "Expand your research" to transition SearchAgent from a search tool into a strategic context layer — keeping users in a single system.

To test the information architecture with the 15 users, I built two concepts. Option A keeps competitor company lists intact, with a "View confirmed customer experts" button next to each company name that reveals experts for that competitor. Option B presents a consolidated confirmed customer view for all competitors, filterable by a specific competitor — arriving pre-filtered when accessed from a competitor's button, but adjustable to show all.

Phase 3 — Operational Migration & V2 Launch

Zero-downtime transition for 700+ associates

Designing the UI was half the battle. The other half: ensuring a zero-downtime transition for all active users. I led technical feasibility workshops to align design with backend constraints, avoided feature creep by anchoring to technical reality early, and met the Q1 release deadline with a 100% stable build.

✦
Key Decision

Descoped Confirmed Customer Lists after engineering flagged months of delay. Research confirmed it wasn't a top user need — delivering the Suggested tab first was the right call. Also descoped the “Expand your research” feature: we decided to run dedicated research to understand the best approach, and the added engineering effort wasn't justified at that stage.

Final Solution

I redesigned SearchAgent's core architecture to eliminate context-switching — bringing customer and competitor research into a single, AI-enhanced workflow.

From Black Box to Glass Box: a discovery engine users actually trust.

Key Design Decisions

Five decisions shaped the V2 experience — each grounded in research findings from 15 global users.

  1. 1
    AI-driven re-ranking Core Innovation

    Used LLMs to interpret briefs and re-rank expert pools by project relevance. The Suggested tab offers a curated starting point ahead of full automation in V3. Rankings incorporate signals such as seniority, tenure, recency, incentive, expert status (prior engagement and recency), and customer familiarity from expert quotes, defined with Data Science.

  2. 2
    Integrated customer research

    Built a direct path to find employees at target firms, bypassing LinkedIn Sales Navigator. Descoped Confirmed Lists for now due to engineering constraints; prioritised NNSP (non-network experts) discovery for immediate competitive advantage — the research showed it served 80% of use cases.

  3. 3
    Explainable AI — transparency as trust −8pp Support Tickets

    Designed transparency disclaimers/tooltips explaining how experts were ranked. Ranking-related support tickets dropped from 10% to 2% post-launch.

  4. 4
    Consolidated Suggested tab

    Surfacing a brief-matched ranked expert list per company, allowing PMs to immediately identify the most relevant and incentivised experts for their first list to the client. This opens the road to the next automation: a shortlist of suggested experts per company, and eventually a project-wide shortlist — making the time-to-first-list even shorter.

Tradeoff — Customer View & NNSP Strategy

The Customer Companies view currently surfaces non-network experts (NNSP) — Dialectica's key competitive advantage in expert recruitment. Confirmed experts from those same companies are listed under each competitor only. Merging both under each customer would have been the ideal UX, but the technical effort wasn't justified by the business value at this stage. This is a deliberate, time-boxed tradeoff to ship V2 on schedule.

Reflections

The biggest design challenge wasn't the interface — it was trust. Associates had built mental models around manual LinkedIn searches because they could control every step. The Suggested tab only worked once users could see exactly why an expert was ranked. Transparency wasn't a nice-to-have; it was the product.

Descoping Confirmed Customer Lists was the right call — but it required convincing stakeholders that partial delivery at Q1 was more valuable than a complete solution at Q3. The research gave me the data to make that argument. Building the evidence base early, before any design work, was what made the tradeoff defensible.

UX as a Revenue Driver

In PE-facing products, design success is measured in commercial velocity. My role was to find the bottleneck — manual search — and use design to break through it.

Simplicity is Earned

V2 started with complex multi-perspective navigation. Through testing with 15 global users, I learned to ruthlessly simplify — one Suggested tab over six separate views.

Trust is the Foundation

Accuracy is useless inside a black box. Collaborating with Data Science on ranking labels ensured users felt in control of the AI — not replaced by it.
